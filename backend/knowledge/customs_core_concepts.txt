================================================================================
CUSTOMS BROKERAGE DOMAIN KNOWLEDGE — CORE CONCEPTS
================================================================================

1. ENTRY NUMBER
   A unique automated identifier for each customs entry, assigned by the
   customs broker (in this dataset, Vandegrift, a Maersk company, with filer
   code 595). An entry number follows the format: filer code (3 digits) +
   entry number (7 digits) + check digit (1 digit). Every shipment clearing
   U.S. customs must have an entry number filed with CBP (Customs and Border
   Protection).

2. ENTRY TYPES
   - Type 01 (Consumption Entry): Standard import entry for goods entering
     U.S. commerce. Accounts for ~95% of all entries.
   - Type 06 (FTZ Entry): Entry of goods admitted to a Foreign Trade Zone.
     Goods may be stored, manipulated, or manufactured in the FTZ before
     formal entry into U.S. commerce. FTZ entries may have different duty
     treatment.

3. ENTRY LIFECYCLE DATES
   - Entry Filed Date: When the customs broker electronically submits the
     entry to CBP via the Automated Commercial Environment (ACE).
   - Release Date: When CBP releases the cargo for delivery. This is the
     canonical date used for most reporting and analytics.
   - Summary Date: When the entry summary (Form 7501) is filed, typically
     10 business days after release. This is when duties are actually paid.

   IMPORTANT: When a user asks about entries "in January" or "for Q1," always
   use Release Date as the filter unless they explicitly specify another date.

4. ENTRY SUMMARY (FORM 7501)
   The official customs declaration document submitted to CBP. Contains all
   tariff information, duties, fees, and taxes due on imported goods. The 7501
   recreates commercial invoice data including invoice numbers, entered values,
   freight charges, MID codes, bills of lading, and detailed tariff line items.

5. BROKER REFERENCE NUMBER
   An internal tracking number assigned by the customs broker (Vandegrift).
   Not the same as the entry number. Used for cross-referencing with internal
   systems and customer communications.

6. BILL OF LADING (BOL / B/L)
   A shipping document issued by the ocean carrier that serves as a receipt
   for cargo, a contract of carriage, and a document of title. Master bills
   cover the full container; house bills may break down to individual
   consignments. The BOL number is critical for tracking shipments.

7. CONTAINER NUMBER
   The unique identifier for each shipping container. Standard format is
   4 letters (owner code + category) + 7 digits. Multiple entries can share
   a container (consolidated shipments), and one entry can span multiple
   containers.

8. MID (MANUFACTURER'S IDENTIFICATION CODE)
   A code constructed from the manufacturer's name and physical address that
   identifies where goods were produced. Required on all customs entries.
   Format: 2-character country code + alphanumeric characters derived from
   the manufacturer name and address. Used for compliance and origin tracking.

9. PORT CODES
   - Port of Entry: The U.S. port where goods are admitted. Common codes
     include 2704 (Los Angeles), 1001 (New York/Newark), 5301 (Houston).
   - Port of Lading: The foreign port where goods were loaded onto the vessel.
   - Port of Unlading: The U.S. port where goods are unloaded from the vessel
     (may differ from port of entry).

10. PAY TYPE
    - Statement: Duties are accumulated and paid periodically (typically
      monthly). Used by importers with CBP-approved periodic payment plans.
    - Daily: Duties are paid at the time of entry summary filing.
